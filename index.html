Welcome to Mei Rose Connor's Website

Hi, I'm Mei Rose, an independent scholar of Formal Logic, a web developer, and a cat lover who owns no cats.

I studied Mathematics and Linguistics in my undergraduate degree program at Stony Brook University, and have now shifted my focus towards finding ways of sharing the joy of Mathematics and Formal Logic with students of all ages through interactive software applications. I like to find the intersections between my interests and the creative arts, and yes, it's true, I love cats but own none.

This site is full of projects in progress, projects completed, and other things I've made or are working on for fun. Feel free to navigate between section using the buttons provided at the top and bottom of every section.

Current Projects

Magic 15 Python Game

This game, isomorphic to tic-tac-toe or Noughts and Crosses, asks two players to take turns choosing numbers between 1 and 9 inclusive with a goal of selecting numbers that sum to 15 before the other player does. It was written in Python and runs on the CMU_Graphics package, which itself is built on the pygame package. Features to be developed include a recusive algorithm for checking whether a win is possible at all after each player takes their turn, and an improved UI.

Kalish-Montague-Mar Natural Deduction LaTeX Formatter

An idea that I've been working on for a while, this code takes a KMM natural deduction given in a plaintext format and turns it into the appropriate boxed-and-cancelled natural deduction one expects from the KMM style, just in a format that is readable into a LaTeX document. So far, it is undecided whether it will do this via TikZ or by producing a vector graphics file. The hard part of this project has been standardizing the plaintext representations of deductions.

Admintopia

Admintopia is a satirical incrememtal clicker game themed around the seemingly-endless number of administrative staff hired at modern American univeristies. The user can click to admit undergraduate students, whose tuition goes solely to paying the salaries of increasingly higher-ranked administrators. Undergraduate student tuition can also pay for Masters students, whose tuition can in turn pay doctoral students. And various numbers of these types of students are required for the "privilege" of hiring higher-ranked admins.
